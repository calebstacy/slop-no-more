# The move catalog

A **move** is a functional unit of discourse: what a clause is *doing*, not what
words it contains. Strings are unbounded; moves are few. AI slop is roughly a
dozen moves wearing ten thousand costumes, so the rules are written at the move
level, where the inventory is small and paraphrase can't escape.

Every entry here is executable three ways with one definition:

1. **Instruction**: given to the generator before writing (raises the pass rate).
2. **Test**: the scanner's pattern family plus this definition as a rubric for
   novel costumes (enforces).
3. **Edit rule**: what to do on failure (repairs).

The critical fact about moves, learned the hard way in live drafting sessions:
**they generate claims.** Each move carries hidden premises, and the generator
backfills assertions to make the premises true. A denial template requires
someone to have made the denied claim, so a claimant gets invented. A "most
teams fail this" template requires a failing population, so a survey that never
happened gets asserted. Removing the move after the fact leaves a hole where
evidence should be, which is why the gate rejects and regenerates rather than
patching.

Lineage: this is metadiscourse and move analysis turned into lint. John
Swales' move analysis (Genre Analysis, 1990) defines a move as a stretch of
discourse doing a particular communicative job, the unit his CARS model
applies to research introductions. Ken Hyland's metadiscourse framework
(Metadiscourse: Exploring Interaction in Writing, 2005) catalogs the
language a text spends on itself: frame markers, engagement markers, boosters,
hedges. This catalog applies both lenses to machine prose, where the
metadiscourse arrives at machine frequency.

Research anchors:

- Kobak, González-Márquez, Horvát, and Lause, "Delving into LLM-assisted
  writing in biomedical publications through excess vocabulary," Science
  Advances 2025 / PubMed PMID 40601754. Their excess-vocabulary method over
  more than 15 million PubMed abstracts found an abrupt post-LLM rise in
  style words and estimated at least 13.5% of 2024 biomedical abstracts had
  LLM processing. This supports the style-marker density layer, not a ban on
  any single word.
- Jiang and Hyland, "Does ChatGPT write like a student? Engagement markers in
  argumentative essays," Written Communication 2025. Their comparison of 145
  ChatGPT essays with 145 student essays found a narrower, less interactive
  engagement profile in the model outputs. This supports the template-roadmap,
  transition, and fake-engagement families.
- Mo and Crosthwaite, "Exploring the affordances of generative AI large
  language models for stance and engagement in academic writing," Journal of
  English for Academic Purposes 2025. Their corpus work found a narrower and
  more repetitive range of stance and engagement features in LLM essays. This
  supports treating repetition of stance frames as a move-level issue.
- Almulla, "The Use of Hedging Devices and Engagement Markers in
  AI-Generated and Human-Written Essays," Open Journal of Modern Linguistics
  2025. Their corpus showed more hedging in AI-generated essays and more
  engagement markers in human essays; the paper cautions that the differences
  were not statistically significant, so hedge-cloud stays medium severity and
  needs pattern density.
- Walters and Wilder, "Fabrication and errors in the bibliographic citations
  generated by ChatGPT," Scientific Reports 2023, plus the 2026 GhostCite
  arXiv study on citation validity. These support the anonymous-authority
  family: authority language must identify the source, not merely resemble
  sourced prose.

## 1. cataphoric-evaluation (high)

**Definition.** An evaluative clause whose referent is upcoming content: the
text praises what it is about to say instead of saying it. The reader is asked
to accept significance on credit.

**Costumes observed.** "Here's why it matters" · "Here is where the money is
going wrong" · "the part worth sitting with" · "worth naming precisely" · "the
key insight is" · "It's important to note that" · sentence-initial "Crucially,"
· the narrative-beat label: "Calibration was the satisfying part" (human catch,
2026-07-01: labels the emotional value of content before delivering it) ·
"It's worth asking where that ends." · "they deserve naming" · "it deserves
to be taken seriously" · "So here is the contract you actually hold" (human
catches, 2026-07-02, essay review: the "deserves" family and the fake-reveal
"here is the X you actually" join the patterns) · "That last part matters" /
"The last part matters" · "The clearest one is" · "That is why I like the word
move" (human catches, 2026-07-02, self-audit of an essay about this very
catalog: old move, new costumes)

**Why it damages the argument.** It substitutes an appraisal for the evidence
that would earn the appraisal, and it commits the piece to a significance the
content then has to live up to. Backwards.

**Instruction.** Never evaluate a point you have not yet made. Lead with the
content; if it matters, the sentence stating it must carry the weight itself.

**Edit rule.** Delete the evaluation, promote the content to the head of the
sentence.

## 2. manufactured-antithesis (high)

**Definition.** A denial of a claim with no attributable claimant, staged so
the correction can carry the assertion. Not-X-but-Y and all kin.

**Costumes observed.** "voice is not a vibe — it is structure you can name" ·
"supplied, not requested" · "not the review, but review that loops"

**Why it damages the argument.** It asserts three unstated premises: that
someone claimed X, that X and Y exclude each other, and that correcting X is
the point. Often the exclusion contradicts the writer's real position (one
caught essay denied voice was "a vibe," then argued one sentence later that
the vibe sits on top of measurable structure).

**Instruction.** Every antithesis must name who asserted the negated half, with
a source. If no one did, state Y as a plain positive claim.

**Edit rule.** Attribute X or convert to the positive claim.

## 3. phantom-population (high)

**Definition.** A quantified claim over a population nobody measured, asserted
because the rhetorical template requires a group that fails or agrees.

**Costumes observed.** "Most teams have a thin answer" · "everyone knows" ·
"nobody talks about" · "we've all seen" · "The replies sorted into two camps.
… The second camp is bigger" (human catch, 2026-07-02: the invented-
cartography variant, which sorts a discourse into tidy camps and then asserts
facts about their sizes that nobody measured)

**Why it damages the argument.** It is a fabricated empirical finding: the
rhetoric runs ahead of the evidence and manufactures data to catch up.

**Instruction.** No quantifier over a population without a source or a number.
First-person scope is always available: "the teams I have seen."

**Edit rule.** Cite it, scope it to first person, or delete it.

## 4. invented-adversary (high)

**Definition.** A rebuttal of an accusation or belief no one voiced, conjured
so the text can perform fairness, generosity, or correction.

**Costumes observed.** "The existing voice guidance is not the villain here" ·
"nobody is saying X" · "skeptics might argue" · "The engineers in the room
have an answer" (human catch, 2026-07-02: "What engineers?" The "Xs in the
room" cast.)

**Why it damages the argument.** It fills the essay with claims about a debate
that does not exist, displacing the real intellectual context.

**Instruction.** Rebut only positions you can quote.

**Edit rule.** Quote the real holder of the position, or delete the rebuttal.

## 5. endophoric-command (high)

**Definition.** An imperative ordering the reader to re-attend to the text
itself, claiming a significance the prose has not earned.

**Costumes observed.** "Read the right column again" · "let that sink in" ·
"sit with that"

**Instruction.** Never direct the reader's attention back at the text. If it
deserves a second read, rewrite until one read lands it.

**Edit rule.** Delete the command.

## 6. false-collaboration (medium)

**Definition.** First-person-plural stagecraft casting the reader as a
participant in work the writer is doing alone.

**Costumes observed.** "Let's unpack this" · "let's be honest" · "we need to
talk about"

**Instruction.** Do the thing instead of announcing it jointly.

**Edit rule.** State the finding; make the argument.

## 7. gatekeeper-test (medium)

**Definition.** Handing the reader a diagnostic question to ask some third
party, casting writer and reader as joint evaluators of an absent population.
Usually imports a consulting register the piece never chose.

**Costumes observed.** "So when a team brings you their setup, ask one thing:"

**Instruction.** Answer your own question, in the text, with your own evidence.

**Edit rule.** Answer it or cut the frame.

## 8. clean-dichotomy (medium)

**Definition.** Asserting that a drawn distinction is sharp as if that were a
fact about the world, when only the sentence is clean.

**Costumes observed.** "That is the line worth drawing, and it cuts cleanly" ·
"there are two kinds of"

**Instruction.** A line earns its sharpness with cases at the boundary. Show
the edge cases or drop the cleanliness claim.

**Edit rule.** Add the boundary cases or soften to what you can defend.

## 9. counterfeit-idiom (high)

**Definition.** A figurative or colloquial phrase that imitates spoken idiom
but is not one anyone says: invented folksiness that signals "human voice"
instead of carrying meaning. The uncanny middle between plain prose and a real
idiom, and a costume of the over-correction register.

**Costumes observed.** "a smell I could point at" (human catch, 2026-07-01,
first specimen) · "em dashes by the pound" · "comes back wearing synonyms" ·
"make the smell pointable" · "little constitution" · "kindness to the reader"

**Adjacent tell, same session.** "Then we gave it teeth" (also a human catch):
an attested idiom, so not counterfeit, but a machine favorite used at a
frequency no writer reaches. Those live in Layer 1 as `stock-idiom`, high
severity: real phrase, fake rate.

**Why it damages the argument.** Nothing reads as fake faster than borrowed
casualness; one counterfeit idiom makes the reader re-audit every sentence
around it.

**Instruction.** Use only idioms you have heard a person say. When in doubt,
say it plainly; the plain phrase is the human one.

**Edit rule.** Replace with the plain statement, or with a real, attested
idiom if one fits.

**Detection note.** Open class: the ear finds these, the scanner only
remembers them. Every human catch gets added as a literal pattern so it can
never ship twice.

## 10. hollow-pivot (high)

**Definition.** A clause styled as a decisive conclusion that only restates or
negates the previous clause: pure rhythm posing as a decision. The sentence
scans as a turning point and contains no information.

**Costumes observed.** "Asking nicely doesn't work, so we stopped asking"
(human catch, 2026-07-01: "doesn't really mean anything, it's empty") ·
"So we did." · "And it worked."

**Why it damages the argument.** It spends the reader's attention at the exact
moment a real decision should appear, and leaves the decision unstated.

**Instruction.** At every turn in the argument, state the decision or
consequence itself. If the punchy version says nothing the prior sentence
didn't, it is not a punchline; it is padding.

**Edit rule.** Replace with the actual decision, or delete.

## 11. dramatized-frame (high)

**Definition.** Verbs and time-scales punched up beyond the facts,
falsifying the writer's actual relationship to the events.

**Costumes observed.** "I spent an afternoon this week interrogating Claude"
(human catch, 2026-07-01: the writer asked, and the thinking was months old,
not a week)

**Why it damages the argument.** It is biography invented for rhythm. The
reader who knows the writer spots it instantly, and it poisons trust in every
factual claim that follows.

**Instruction.** Use the literal verb and the real timeline. Drama the facts
did not supply is fiction.

**Edit rule.** Restore the plain verb; restore the actual time-scale.

**Detection note.** Ear-first, like counterfeit-idiom: only the writer knows
their own facts. Catches accumulate as literal patterns.

## 12. unanchored-we (medium)

**Definition.** A first-person-plural actor in single-author prose whose
members are never named, blurring who actually did what.

**Costumes observed.** "We wrote definitions for nine of these moves" (human
catch, 2026-07-01: "'We'? who is we?")

**Why it damages the argument.** Agency is information. "We built" hides
whether the writer directed, built, or watched, and the reader can feel the
dodge.

**Instruction.** Name the actors: I decided, the model built. Reserve "we"
for a group the reader can identify.

**Edit rule.** Replace each "we" with the named actor who did that specific
thing.

## 13. borrowed-inference (high)

**Definition.** An inferential connective (so, which means, if X then Y,
therefore) asserting a logical relation the text never establishes. Logic's
syntax used as transition music.

**Costumes observed.** "The instrument agrees with my ear. And if the ear can
be automated, it can be pointed at bigger things" (human catch, 2026-07-01:
"why in the world would doing x mean y?")

**Why it damages the argument.** It is the argument-level twin of the
counterfeit idiom: the sentence performs reasoning instead of doing it. One
unearned "which means" teaches the reader to distrust every connective in the
piece. It is an operator with no operands.

**Instruction.** Every inferential connective must be backed by reasoning
present in the text. If the second claim is really a plan, a hope, or a
separate fact, say so plainly instead of disguising it as a conclusion.

**Edit rule.** Supply the missing reasoning, or drop the connective and
reclassify the claim honestly.

**Detection note.** Ear-first. The reliable grep-able costume is the
pseudo-syllogism ("if X can be Aed, it can be Bed"); most instances need a
reader asking "does Y actually follow?"

## 14. empty-emphasis (medium)

**Definition.** An intensity claim about the writer's own argument doing work
that evidence should do.

**Costumes observed.** "This feels responsible. It is partly a trap." · "That
is the real waste" · "the scariest class" · "This is the whole bet" · "That
is the threshold I care about" · "That is technical work"

**Instruction.** Replace the intensity word with the fact that justifies it.

**Edit rule.** If no fact justifies it, the sentence goes.

## 15. count-contrast-lockup (high)

**Definition.** A verbless apposition of counted noun phrases, "N xs, one y,"
or its inversion, deployed as a heading, kicker, or closer so the count
rhythm can assert a synthesis (many parts, one whole) the prose has not
established.

**Costumes observed.** "Three jobs, one system" (portfolio-draft case-study
heading, human catch 2026-07-02) · "One argument, four parts" (same draft,
same catch) · "Two channels, two different ingestion methods, one vocabulary"
(same project's notes) · "One failure string, eight regenerations" (a
component header; the rhythm fires even where the counts are literal)

**Why it damages the argument.** The "one y" arrives with no mechanism:
nothing in the sentence says what unifies the parts, so unity is asserted by
cadence alone. And the cell is a recognizable machine rhythm, kin to the
triad, so it reads as generated even when the claim is true.

**Legitimate neighbors, excluded by the definition.** Partitive counts of the
same noun ("nine drafts, one past the gate") and flat data inventories of
three or more counts ("one task, two models, fifty runs per cell") state
facts rather than synthesis. The pattern family carves these out; for novel
cases the test is whether the final count claims a whole the prose never
assembled.

**Instruction.** Never pair counts to imply convergence. If parts genuinely
form one system, write the sentence that says how.

**Edit rule.** Replace the lockup with a heading that names what the list is,
or with the sentence stating the unifying mechanism. The unity claim moves
into prose that can carry it, or it goes.

## 16. phantom-bargain (high)

**Definition.** A mechanism recast as a deal, promise, or contract between
the reader and an abstraction, so the argument can borrow the authority of an
agreement no one made. The register leans in close and levels with you about
terms that were invented one sentence earlier.

**Costumes observed.** "So here is the contract you actually hold" · "The
model promises you a fluent sample … a promise about delivery, never about
meaning" · "English's deal is that someone makes meaning of it" (all three
human catches, 2026-07-02, in one essay; the reviewer's notes: "talking AT
me, not to me … nudging you and trying to level with you," and "What do you
mean 'English's deal'?")

**Why it damages the argument.** It generates claims: the bargain's terms are
asserted as if agreed, and the reader is enrolled as a counterparty to a
contract that doesn't exist. A model promises nothing; sampling returns a
draw. The leveling posture makes it worse: intimacy in service of an
invented obligation.

**Legitimate neighbor.** Design-by-contract and API-contract language in
technical writing describes real, documented interfaces. The move is the
*personified* bargain with the reader, not the CS term.

**Instruction.** Name the mechanism and who actually does what. No contract
language without an actual agreement.

**Edit rule.** Replace the bargain with the mechanism: what is produced, by
what process, with what guarantees actually documented. If the sentence
survives only as a deal, it goes.

## 17. anonymous-authority (high)

**Definition.** A source-shaped claim that invokes research, evidence,
experts, or consensus without naming the source. It borrows the look of
verification while withholding the verifiable object.

**Costumes observed.** "Research shows" · "studies suggest" · "evidence
indicates" · "experts agree" · "it is widely recognized" · "a growing body of
research"

**Why it damages the argument.** It is a citation-shaped phantom. The sentence
asks the reader to grant authority, but gives them no author, date, study,
sample, method, or disputed boundary to inspect. In AI prose this is the
low-friction cousin of hallucinated citation: the text may not invent a
bibliographic entry, but it invents the felt presence of one.

**Instruction.** No authority without an authority. Name the source and the
claim it supports, or write the sentence as your own bounded claim.

**Edit rule.** Add the source and make the supported claim precise, or delete
the authority wrapper and lower the confidence.

**Legitimate neighbor.** A sourced sentence with a real citation, URL, DOI,
PMID, arXiv id, or nearby named source. The scanner suppresses this move when
that evidence appears on the same line; a human reviewer still verifies the
source actually says what the prose claims.

## 18. hedge-cloud (medium)

**Definition.** A stack of modal and hedging terms that diffuses
responsibility for a claim instead of calibrating uncertainty.

**Costumes observed.** "may suggest a potential pathway" · "could possibly
indicate" · "generally tends to" · "arguably may" · "appears likely to"

**Why it damages the argument.** Hedging is legitimate when it tells the reader
where uncertainty lives. A hedge-cloud does the opposite: it fogs the verb so
the writer never has to name the sample limit, source quality, time horizon, or
degree of confidence.

**Instruction.** Use one uncertainty term and attach it to the reason for
uncertainty. "Probably" without a why is padding.

**Edit rule.** Keep the strongest accurate modal, then state the uncertainty's
source: small sample, indirect evidence, early data, disputed mechanism, or
first-person scope.

## 19. transition-turnstile (medium)

**Definition.** A sentence or paragraph begins with a formal transition that
supplies motion without a real relation between claims.

**Costumes observed.** "Moreover," · "Furthermore," · "Additionally," ·
"In addition," · "Overall," · "Ultimately," · sentence-initial "Notably,"
"Importantly," and "Crucially,"

**Why it damages the argument.** The transition promises architecture. If the
relation is only "another sentence is coming," the text has replaced thinking
with hallway signage.

**Instruction.** Use a transition only when you can name the relation:
contrast, cause, exception, consequence, narrower case, wider case, or return.

**Edit rule.** Delete the transition or replace it with the actual relation.
If no relation exists, reorder or split the paragraph.

## 20. template-roadmap (medium)

**Definition.** The text announces the generic task it will perform instead of
beginning the task with a substantive claim.

**Costumes observed.** "This essay explores" · "This article examines" ·
"In this guide, we will" · "This piece is about how" · "This section unpacks"

**Why it damages the argument.** It spends the first sentence proving the
piece knows what genre it is in. Human drafts can contain scaffolding; finished
prose should not leave the scaffolding as the opening move unless the roadmap
itself is useful information.

**Instruction.** Start with the first claim, example, question, or scene that
belongs to this specific piece.

**Edit rule.** Cut the roadmap. Promote the first non-generic sentence, or
write the claim the roadmap was avoiding.

## 21. coverage-sweep (medium)

**Definition.** A breadth montage that gestures across a range instead of
selecting the specific cases the argument needs.

**Costumes observed.** "everything from X to Y" · "ranging from X to Y" ·
"spanning X to Y" · "across a wide range of" · "a broad array of"

**Why it damages the argument.** Breadth can impersonate evidence. The sweep
suggests the writer has surveyed the territory, but often no selection
principle exists. The result is a panoramic sentence where a case should be.

**Instruction.** Do not gesture at coverage. Choose the cases that bear the
claim and say why those cases matter.

**Edit rule.** Replace the sweep with two or three named cases, plus the
selection rule. If the exact cases do not matter, cut the sweep.

## 22. sterile-balance (medium)

**Definition.** A symmetrical concession frame that performs neutrality
without adjudicating which side is true, stronger, or relevant.

**Costumes observed.** "while there are challenges, it also offers
opportunities" · "both promise and peril" · "not without its limitations" ·
"presents both benefits and risks"

**Why it damages the argument.** It produces the tone of fairness without the
work of judgment. The sentence often invents an equally weighted tradeoff
because the model's default register wants to sound measured.

**Instruction.** Do not balance by reflex. Name the actual tradeoff, weight
the sides, and attach the judgment to evidence.

**Edit rule.** Replace with a weighted tradeoff: "X matters more than Y
because Z." If no weighting is available, write the narrower claim and drop
the ceremonial caveat.

## 23. benefit-cascade (medium)

**Definition.** A chain of generic uplift verbs that converts desired outcomes
into evidence of mechanism.

**Costumes observed.** "enhance efficiency and foster collaboration" ·
"drive engagement and promote growth" · "empower users to streamline outcomes"

**Why it damages the argument.** Benefit verbs are conclusions wearing work
boots. They say what the writer hopes will happen, then smuggle that hope into
the causal layer as if the mechanism had been shown.

**Instruction.** No benefit stack without mechanism. Name who does what
differently and what changes because of it.

**Edit rule.** Replace the cascade with the mechanism, or with one concrete
outcome and the evidence that supports it.

## Adding a move

The discipline that keeps this catalog honest:

1. **Define functionally first.** One sentence, in the form "a clause that
   [does X] instead of / in place of [Y]." If you can only list phrases, you
   have a string rather than a move; put it in Layer 1.
2. **Then collect costumes** from real specimens (file:line), and write the
   pattern family from the costumes.
3. **Write the edit rule as a repair, not a ban.** The rule must say what the
   sentence becomes, because moves are load-bearing: deleting one leaves a hole
   where evidence should be, and the rule has to say what fills it.
4. **State why it damages the argument.** If you cannot, it may be a style
   preference rather than a move, and this catalog does not do style preferences.

## Known limits

- The scanner catches known costumes deterministically. A genuinely novel
  costume needs the definition applied as a rubric (by a reviewer or a
  classifier pass) and then gets added as a pattern, so the deterministic
  layer grows over time.
- Attributed, sourced antithesis is legitimate rhetoric. The edit rule (name
  the claimant) is the test; `slop-ignore` marks the deliberate cases.
- Calibration (2026-07-01): the essay a human reader flagged as painful scored
  53.9 weighted density; the draft that passed the same reader's voice gate
  scored 0.0. The instrument and the ear agreed on rank order at first try.
